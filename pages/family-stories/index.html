Family Stories & Oral Histories

Explore personal accounts, memories, and stories passed down through generations of Hatfield and McCoy descendants.

About These Stories

The stories presented here have been collected from descendants of both the Hatfield and McCoy families over many decades, providing personal perspectives on the feud and its aftermath.

These oral histories offer unique insights into how the feud has been remembered and interpreted by those with direct family connections to the events. They reveal how the conflict affected individual lives and shaped family identities across generations.

While these accounts may sometimes differ from documented historical facts, they represent an important dimension of the feud's legacy - how it lives on in family memory and storytelling traditions.

A Note on These Accounts

The stories presented here are personal recollections and family traditions, not verified historical documents. They represent how events were remembered and passed down through generations, sometimes decades after they occurred.

Where possible, we have noted when accounts differ significantly from documented historical records. These differences themselves are valuable, as they show how memory and storytelling shape our understanding of the past.

Oral History Collection

These oral histories were recorded in interviews with Hatfield and McCoy descendants from the 1950s through the early 2000s.

Featured Stories

The New Year's Night Attack

Told by Martha McCoy Johnson (granddaughter of Randolph McCoy), Recorded 1952

My grandmother never spoke much about that night, but my father told me what he remembered. He was just a boy when it happened. It was bitterly cold, he said, with snow on the ground. The family had gone to bed after celebrating the New Year when they heard the dogs barking.

My great-grandfather Randolph got up to see what was happening. That's when the shooting started. The Hatfields had surrounded the house. My father said his mother pushed him and his siblings under a bed while Randolph tried to defend the home.

The most terrible part was when they set the house on fire. My aunt Alifair was shot when she stepped outside, and my uncle Calvin was killed too. My grandmother Sarah was beaten so badly she never fully recovered. Randolph escaped into the woods, but he lost everything that night - his home, two of his children, and in many ways, his wife too.

Devil Anse's Conversion

Told by William Hatfield (grandson of Devil Anse Hatfield), Recorded 1965

Grandfather was a changed man after his baptism in 1911. The family was shocked when he announced he wanted to be baptized. He'd never been a religious man during the feud years - quite the opposite. But something changed in him as he got older.

He told my father that he'd been having dreams about judgment day. In these dreams, he said he could see all the men who had died during the feud years, and they were pointing at him. After these dreams started, he couldn't sleep through the night.

The day of his baptism, hundreds of people came to see it. Nobody could believe that Devil Anse Hatfield was being baptized. They held it in Main Island Creek, and grandfather walked into that cold water wearing a white shirt. When he came out, he told everyone that the feud was truly over for him.

Working with the Enemy

Told by Frankie McCoy Williams (featured in the 1944 Life Magazine article), Recorded 1978

When I first met Shirley Hatfield at the factory in Detroit, I didn't tell her my name right away. I knew who she was as soon as I heard her last name and where she was from. I was nervous about how she might react when she found out I was a McCoy.

We worked side by side for two weeks before I finally told her. I remember saying, "You know, my maiden name is McCoy." She just looked at me for a moment, then laughed and said, "Well, I guess we're supposed to be feuding, aren't we?"

After that, we became good friends. We were both far from home, working to support the war effort. When the Life Magazine people came around looking for a story, someone told them about us. We didn't think it would be such a big deal, but they made us pose together and took all those pictures.

Family Reunions & Reconciliation

Since the mid-20th century, descendants of the Hatfield and McCoy families have held joint reunions and reconciliation events, symbolizing the end of the historic feud.

1976

First Major Reunion

Descendants of both families gathered in Pikeville, Kentucky for the first major public reconciliation event, coinciding with the American Bicentennial celebrations.

2000

Formal Peace Treaty

Representatives of both families signed a symbolic peace treaty, officially ending the feud. The ceremony was attended by dozens of descendants and covered by national media.

2003

Joint Family Cookbook

Descendants collaborated to create "The Hatfield & McCoy Reunion Cookbook," featuring family recipes and stories from both sides of the feud.

2018

History Channel Premiere

Descendants from both families attended the premiere of the History Channel's miniseries about the feud, providing their perspectives on how the story was portrayed.
