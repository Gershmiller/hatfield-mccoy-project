Research Integrity

The Hatfield-McCoy Feud Research Database is committed to maintaining the highest standards of historical integrity. This page outlines our approach to research, verification, and citation practices.

Our Academic Standards

Verification Process

All information presented in this database undergoes a rigorous verification process:

  • Cross-referencing multiple primary sources when available
  • Consulting historical publications and peer-reviewed research
  • Distinguishing between verified facts and historical inferences
  • Explicitly marking gaps in historical knowledge
  • Consulting with historical experts when necessary

Citation Standards

We adhere to strict citation standards throughout the database:

  • All factual claims are supported by citations to primary or secondary sources
  • Primary sources are prioritized whenever possible
  • Chicago Manual of Style is used as our default citation format
  • Full bibliographic information is provided for all sources
  • Direct quotations are clearly marked and attributed

Handling Historical Uncertainty

The Hatfield-McCoy feud, like many historical events, contains areas of uncertainty and conflicting accounts. We address these challenges through:

Language of Certainty

We use specific language to indicate levels of historical certainty:

  • Verified fact: "Devil Anse Hatfield died on January 6, 1921."
  • Strong evidence: "Evidence strongly suggests that the Hatfields were responsible for the New Year's Night attack."
  • Competing accounts: "Accounts differ on who fired the first shot, with Hatfield sources claiming..."
  • Historical inference: "Based on the pattern of events, historians infer that economic competition was a significant factor."
  • Unknown: "The exact location of this meeting remains unknown."

Presenting Multiple Perspectives

When historical accounts conflict, we:

  • Present multiple perspectives with their respective sources
  • Avoid privileging one family's account over the other
  • Consider the biases and limitations of each source
  • Acknowledge when definitive conclusions cannot be drawn
  • Provide context for why accounts might differ

Primary vs. Secondary Sources

Primary Sources

Primary sources are materials created during the time period under study or by participants in the events described:

  • Contemporary newspaper articles (1863-1891)
  • Court records and legal documents
  • Letters and personal correspondence
  • Photographs from the period
  • Maps and property records
  • First-person accounts and interviews

Primary sources provide direct evidence but may contain biases and inaccuracies that require critical evaluation.

Secondary Sources

Secondary sources are interpretations and analyses created after the events by non-participants:

  • Academic books and journal articles
  • Historical analyses and biographies
  • Documentary films with expert commentary
  • Museum exhibits and educational materials
  • Later newspaper retrospectives

Secondary sources provide context and interpretation but should be evaluated based on their use of primary sources and scholarly rigor.

Avoiding Common Pitfalls in Feud Research

Challenges We Address

  • Sensationalism: We avoid sensationalizing violence or perpetuating stereotypes about Appalachian culture.
  • Folklore vs. Fact: We distinguish between documented events and folklore that developed around the feud.
  • Family Bias: We recognize that many accounts come from family members with inherent biases.
  • Presentism: We avoid judging historical actions by contemporary standards without proper context.
  • Oversimplification: We acknowledge the complex social, economic, and political factors that contributed to the feud.
  • Cultural Stereotyping: We present Appalachian culture with respect and nuance, avoiding "hillbilly" stereotypes.

Academic Use Statement

The Hatfield-McCoy Feud Research Database is designed for educational and research purposes. When using information from this database in historical work:

  • Cite this database as a source using the citation format required by your institution
  • Follow the original source citations provided to consult primary materials directly when possible
  • Apply critical thinking and source evaluation to all historical information
  • Consider consulting multiple sources to verify important facts
  • Acknowledge areas of historical uncertainty in your own work

Suggested citation format for this database:
