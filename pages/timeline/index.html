Interactive Timeline

Explore the dual timeline of the Hatfield-McCoy feud, covering both the original feud period (1863-1891) and its cultural legacy through the mid-20th century (1940s-1950s). Use the filters to focus on specific aspects of the feud or toggle between time periods.

Original Feud Period (1863-1891)

1863

Civil War Service

Asa Harmon McCoy joins the Union Army during the Civil War, while Devil Anse Hatfield forms a Confederate guerrilla group called the "Logan Wildcats."

View Details
January 1865

Murder of Asa Harmon McCoy

Asa Harmon McCoy is murdered after returning home from Union Army service. While Devil Anse Hatfield is suspected, the murder is likely committed by his uncle Jim Vance.

View Details
1878

Dispute Over Floyd Hatfield's Pig

Randolph McCoy accuses Floyd Hatfield of stealing his hog, leading to a trial where Bill Staton (related to both families) testifies in favor of the Hatfields.

View Details
1880

Romance Between Roseanna McCoy and Johnse Hatfield

Roseanna McCoy and Johnse Hatfield begin a relationship at an election day gathering, with Roseanna leaving her family to live with the Hatfields.

View Details
August 1882

Election Day Fight

Ellison Hatfield is stabbed 26 times and shot by three McCoy brothers (Tolbert, Pharmer, and Bud) during an election day dispute.

View Details
August 9, 1882

Execution of McCoy Brothers

After Ellison Hatfield dies from his wounds, Devil Anse Hatfield and his men tie the three McCoy brothers to pawpaw bushes and execute them by firing squad.

View Details
1887-1888

Legal Battle Between Kentucky and West Virginia

Kentucky Governor Simon Buckner requests extradition of Hatfields to Kentucky. West Virginia Governor E. Willis Wilson refuses, leading to a legal battle that reaches the U.S. Supreme Court.

View Details
January 1, 1888

New Year's Night Massacre

Jim Vance leads a Hatfield party in an attack on the McCoy cabin, killing Calvin and Alifair McCoy and severely beating Sarah McCoy. The cabin is set on fire.

View Details
1889-1890

Trials of the Hatfields

Nine Hatfields and their supporters are tried for the murder of the McCoy family members. Seven receive life imprisonment, and Ellison Mounts is sentenced to death.

View Details
February 18, 1890

Execution of Ellison Mounts

Ellison Mounts is hanged in Pikeville, Kentucky, marking the last formal execution of the feud. His last words reportedly were "The Hatfields made me do it."

View Details

Mid-Century Legacy (1940s-1950s)

May 1944

Life Magazine Article

Life Magazine publishes "Hatfields and McCoys now fight together in the Army, work together in mines and factories in their oldtime feuding territory," featuring Shirley Hatfield and Frankie McCoy working together in a WWII uniform factory.

View Details
1946

First Hatfield-McCoy Reunion

The first official reunion of the Hatfield and McCoy families is held, symbolizing reconciliation between the descendants of the feuding families.

View Details
1949

Publication of "The Hatfields and the McCoys"

Virgil Carrington Jones publishes "The Hatfields and the McCoys," one of the first comprehensive historical accounts of the feud, bringing renewed national attention to the story.

View Details
1950

Warner Bros. "Hillbilly Hare" Released

Warner Bros. releases the Bugs Bunny cartoon "Hillbilly Hare," featuring feuding hillbilly brothers clearly inspired by the Hatfield-McCoy feud, popularizing the feud in American pop culture.

View Details
1951

"Comin' Round the Mountain" Film

Universal Pictures releases "Comin' Round the Mountain," a comedy film starring Abbott and Costello that features a fictional version of the Hatfield-McCoy feud.

View Details
1955

CBS Television Dramatization

CBS airs a dramatized account of the feud on its "You Are There" historical reenactment series, bringing the story to television audiences across America.

How This Timeline Was Created

This interactive timeline was developed using a rigorous historical methodology:

  • All events are verified through multiple primary and secondary sources
  • Dates are presented as specifically as historical records allow
  • When exact dates are unknown, we provide the most accurate timeframe possible
  • Events are categorized by family involvement, type, and historical significance
  • The dual timeline structure highlights both the original feud and its cultural legacy
